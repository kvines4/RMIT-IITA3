Overview

Our idea is to create a fitness/strength training-focused mobile app that will use your phone's built-in camera to watch you do compound lifts such as squats, bench-press or deadlifts and analyse your lifting form. This app would use real-time image recognition software to track information about the effort exerted in each lift. It will also provide insight into the centre of mass of the barbell and its motion over time; the user's body proportions, such as how far you are leaning forward and how deep your lift is. The application will then provide information on how safe your posture is. To analyse a user's form, we will use a scale of difficulty called RPE (rating of perceived effort) where zero would be at rest, five might be challenging to lift, and 10 is our absolute maximum weight lift. Server-side processing will be used for all information gathering and then displayed locally on the app on your device.

Looking at the bigger picture, our project is designed to help users gain confidence in their ability no matter where they start. Whether you have been training for years or thinking about starting to train, our application will ensure that the exercises you complete are completed with the proper form and suitable weight to ensure positive results. We hope users can develop their fitness/strength training knowledge using our application. Creating this application will help users ensure they are not living a sedentary lifestyle and promote a healthy, balanced and active lifestyle instead.

Project Plan

Aims

The aim of this project is "Learn correct form at the gym". Learning the correct from at the gym through an app has multiple steps to complete before the correct form is achieved

  • Record yourself doing the exercise (squat, bench press, deadlift etc)

This part is a vital part of the app's functionality, as filming the user doing the exercise is going to give the app the data it will need to be able to analyse the correct form. The goal is that the user learns the correct lifting technique and will no longer receive notifications on how to correct their form, as it will be perfected while using the app.

  • Wait for the app to analyse the video and read the prompts to correct the form

In this step the app will scan the video of the user doing the exercise and give prompts and tips for the user on how to correct their form. Such as, "keep your back straight", "keep your neck straight", or "point your knees out". After this the user would repeat step 1 and take into consideration the prompts the app provided and try to apply them in the next set. This step will provide information on the correct technique for the user so they can consider this in their next set. The goal is for the user to fix the motion while doing the exercise.

  • Track your progress

The app also provides an option to track the weight used and sets completed so that the user can follow their progress week by week and progressively overload to make sure they are making progress at the gym. The goal is that the user will continuously improve their performance at the gym by getting stronger and improving their technique.

The most important steps would be 1 and 2, as the main idea of the app is to have the app record motion while the user is performing the exercise and analysing it, and for the user to correct their form after the app has finished the analysis. Step 3 is not compulsory and can be optional if the user wishes to track their strength progress.

Context

How it began:

Many apps are available today focused on health and fitness, with a handful being centrally focused around using a camera to analyse a person while they lift. The idea for our application comes from a noticeable need for features around safety and lifting technique as well as performance analysis, with the focus being on strength training and powerlifting. The current selection of apps available today are mostly focused on bodybuilding and/or yoga.

Compared to bodybuilding where the main idea is to complete many reps at a lower weight to cause localized muscle damage that will promote muscle growth; powerlifting is about training the body to better activate your muscles to lift increasingly larger and larger weights. Due to the massively increased time spent lifting very heavy weights, a powerlifter is more at risk of hurting themselves if they lift with incorrect or outright dangerous technique.

The main concepts involved in lifting safety are ensuring you always have a good centre of gravity, ensuring the bar follows a straight up and down path, and ensuring you do not arch your back dangerously when under load. These safety factors can be hard to keep track of when lifting close to your maximum weight, which is why many athletes will record their gym sessions for later review by either themselves or their coaches.

By creating an app that focuses more on compound lift technique and less around tracking reps and calories, we can assist athletes of any skill level with ensuring they are always lifting safely at times when they do not have professional coaching available as an option.

Additionally, when training at an athlete level, it is important to have back and forth feedback with your coach about how training is going between PT sessions and by tracking things such as the bar movement path and speed at various weights, we can provide coaches with a tangible way of measuring performance with statistics instead of from the athlete describing how they felt during the session.

Testing

Testing is crucial aspect of software development and can directly affect all aspects of development from the speed of releases and to the overall success of a new release.

The two main categories of testing are automated and manual testing. Manual testing is done buy a person who is actively using the app, often in the context of checking the overall user experience is good and assuring that the basic business requirements are being met from the perspective of those using the application. Automated testing typically refers to the many methods of using testing code to automatically check various aspects of your project from the results returned from your API’s, the integrity of your validation code and the interactions between your different modules such as the front end connecting to API which in turn connects to the database.

Unit Testing is the simplest form of automated testing we can implement and should always be added from the very beginning. Unit tests are typically pretty low level and are used to test code in isolation from the rest of the system; For example, if we have a function call that is meant to return a set of data based on a given user ID, a simple unit test we can add might pass in a valid user and assert that some valid data is received in return, or to pass in an invalid user id and assert that the error is handled properly and correct error data is returned. Unit testing can be done by a user running software on their local machine, but can also be tied into the build process itself; i.e., if any unit tests fail, the build process is halted and the engineers are informed. This will be our main form of testing in the early stages of development while building our API.

Figure 1: An example of a function with its associated Unit Test and its result

Integration tests and Functional tests have a broader focus than unit testing. For example, if we have an application which is installed on device X, which connects to an API that is hosted on server Y, which then connects to the database hosted on server Z, we need to know that all of these modules are talking to each other successfully. This is where integration testing comes in. Functional testing on the other hand is more focused around the overall business/user experience and whether the output matches what is expected during typical use and less so on the state of individual modules.

A further extension of functional testing is end-to-end testing. This is meant to completely replicate how a user would experience the application and can be either manually tested or done automatically. A typical end-to-end test might be to open the application and log into your user account. Open pages x,y and z, change some settings and then make a post. End-to-end testing is a lot harder/more expensive to maintain as an application evolves. In the early stages of development, these jobs will typically be small enough that they can be done manually by the developers as they build the functionality, but as complexity grows and the project matures, we will need to start automating some of this process and have some dedicated testers.

Another important aspect of testing a large-scale project is performance testing. How many users are using the application at once? How many API calls are made in a typical session on the app? And how many of these calls are doing read or write operations to the database? Performance testing answers a lot of these questions with a good degree of accuracy and allow for proper planning/maintenance of networking infrastructure. A fantastic example of a great performance testing tool is Microsoft Azure’s Monitoring tool, which has features such as Application Insights, Log Analytics and VM Insights.

The Azure Monitor tracks and analyses all telemetry from your cloud and on-premises environments and uses tooling to provide helpful real time information and advice on where bottlenecks are, which resources can be expanded such as the number of VMs running your services. It can also build up a visualisation of all the components of your project and provide information on how the interact with each other,

Performance testing like this is an important stage to keep in mind as we are developing our infrastructure. We don’t want to undercut the performance of our application to save money on resources, but we also don’t want to fire up too many unnecessary VMs if we don’t need to. Performance testing will be done from the beginning as we start to build up our testing environments and move into production.

Risks

With anything in life, a certain amount of risk is involved in any action, and when it comes to creating our project, it is no different. Therefore Risk management is key to ensuring our project is a great success. Common risks such as computers breaking down, software not working properly, and staff health issues are generic risks. These risks will still affect the project, however below are the risks that are more relatable to our project:

Creating an application that does not work: As we have promised to overdeliver in this project with the features we have, we must be able to create and run the features we have promised our users and investors. If the application fails to have the features we promised, it will pose a significant threat regarding how profitable and usable the application is.

Poor UX/UI: If we can integrate our promised features into the application, this will be great; however, the application needs to run the software for the application in a timely and smooth manner. Failure to do this will result in an application that is buggy and slow, which means users will less likely use the application and leave bad reviews for it. This will then bring the profitability of the application, which will displease users and investors. In the UX/UI development phase of our application, the developers must spend time and effort creating a smooth and bug-free application; otherwise risk facing the consequences.

Data security risks: When storing data and especially user data, we must outline security measures to avoid any data breaches through cyberattacks or internal leaks. Therefore the project team must involve cybersecurity specialists to constantly patch the software, protect the IT infrastructure and its assets and also monitor any data breaches. Failure to do so means that we lose important user data to cyber-attacks, which will result in a loss of trust from users and investors and essentially ruin our project.

Injuring oneself while using our application: As our project is based around the health and fitness industry and requires users to perform exercises, we will need to create extensive terms and conditions, which users must agree on before using the application. This is because if a user is to hurt themselves using the application, they may be entitled to compensation from us as we are informing the user on how to do an exercise. If the project is taken to court for injury compensation, it may result in the project going bust for the payout/s.

Group processes and communications

Our group has chosen to communicate through Microsoft teams with clearly structured 30-minute to 1-hour long meetings. Attendance at these meetings, held weekly, often on Wednesday, is the absolute bare minimum requirement that our group has set as a compulsory standard. In the initial project meetings, requirements are run through all together as a group with an outline of delegated tasks and responsibilities are discussed and documented so that the entire group is on the same page. In conjunction with this, the group discusses and agrees upon a set timeframe and timeline of tasks and when they can be expected to be finished. We set firm midway project check-in where 50% of the project is required, a soft check-in date where the project is set to be finished and then we have the final deadline as a worst-case submission time if anything needs to be fixed up. These meetings are recorded for members that were not able to be present, with an automatically generated transcript also available. At the end of each meeting, Will goes over the meeting notes and outlines the updates in weekly delegated responsibilities. 

In addition to this, we also use a group chat on Microsoft teams to communicate with each other about any updates, questions and concerns we might have. This space also facilitates informal contributions to documents that can aid in improving the document or provide further context for the other group members to help contribute to their delegated sections and responsibilities.

Should there be a member of the group that is found to not be pulling their weight or completely fails to meet expectations by primarily the midway check in but also including the soft due date, mitigation by a third-party representative is sought out to help provide a solution. This process is aimed to be sought out shortly after the midway check-in if the group member has failed to meet expectations and/or the group has lost communication with said member. That being said, if this were to happen, the ideal scenario would be that the group member is reminded and notified of the expectation and requirements agreed upon at the start of the project. They are then given 24-48 hours to meet requirements without a group representative having to reach out to a third-party representative. In the event of unexpected loss of communication with a group member, a group representative is to attempt to make contact at minimum by a private message on Microsoft Teams and via email to the member's student email before seeking alternate arrangements.

All work is to be produced in the shared drive that all group members have access to and is stored in the cloud for version history. Within this drive, the group members are to work solely on the specific projects master document and house any files/attachments in folders within the shared drive. This provides the entire group with the same content, file history of members' contributions for tracking changes, and redundancy from a single point of failure. The excuse of losing work that was not saved within the drive is not accepted and is rejected by the group as in the case the member has failed to follow production procedure as agreed upon by all members of the project.
